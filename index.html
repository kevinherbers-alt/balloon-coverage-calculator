How the balloon count is calculated

Every shape reduces to a length or area to cover, multiplied by how many balloons fit per foot at your chosen fullness. A classic garland uses clusters of four 11" balloons per foot. Organic styles pack more in: mixed sizes (16" statement balloons, 11" workhorses, and 5" fillers tucked into the gaps) are what create the full, flowing look — and they raise the count fast. Arches and hoops first convert their dimensions into a linear length (an arch over a doorway is roughly its width plus twice its height along the curve; a hoop is just its circumference), and walls work by the square foot instead.

The result always includes a 10% buffer, because balloons pop during inflation, necks tear while tying, and colors run short — every experienced decorator orders extra. The breakdown by size is what you actually put in your cart.

Measuring tips from decorators

  • Garlands: don't guess the length — run a string or painter's tape along the actual path (up a stair rail, across a mantel, around a doorway) and measure the string.
  • Number and letter frames: measure the outline path the balloons will follow, including inner cutouts (the hole in a 0 or 8 counts). Mosaic-style frames filled with 5" balloons use far more than you'd expect.
  • Walls: width × height in feet. If only part of the wall gets balloons, measure just that section.
  • Balloon sizes are inflated sizes. An "11-inch balloon" under-inflated to 8 or 9 inches packs tighter and needs more per foot — when in doubt, choose the fuller style option.
