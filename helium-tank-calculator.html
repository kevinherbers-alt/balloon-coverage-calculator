How much helium each balloon uses

Helium usage grows fast with balloon size, because volume scales with the cube of the diameter: a 9" latex balloon uses roughly 0.25 cubic feet, an 11" about 0.5, a 16" about 1.5, and a 36" jumbo a whopping 15 cubic feet — one jumbo drinks as much helium as thirty 11" balloons. An 18" foil balloon uses about 0.4 cubic feet. This calculator sums your counts and adds a 10% allowance for topping off, valve loss, and the tank pressure you can never quite fully use.

Disposable tank or rented cylinder?

The small disposable tanks sold at party stores (typically 8.9 cu ft, marketed as "fills ~30 balloons" — true for 9-inch, closer to 15–18 for 11-inch) are convenient but the most expensive helium per cubic foot. If your design needs more than two or three of them, calling a welding-gas or party supply company about a rented cylinder is nearly always cheaper. Rentals also fill balloons faster and to more consistent sizes.

Do you even need helium?

Worth asking before you spend: garlands, arches on frames, columns, walls, and number frames are almost always air-filled — no helium at all, and they last days instead of hours. Helium is for floating designs: ceiling balloons, string-of-pearls arches, balloon bouquets and releases. If your project came from our coverage calculator, chances are it's an air-filled design and your helium bill is zero.
